> If the final destination of realization is your own Self then what is the path to get there?
>
> Can you walk towards your own feet?
{: .prompt-tip }

## Becoming

Much of ordinary life is organized around the desire to become something other than what we currently take ourselves to be. We seek achievement, recognition, security, emotional certainty, and personal improvement because we often assume that peace belongs to a future version of ourselves. This tendency can quietly enter spiritual life as well. Meditation, study, ritual, discipline, and reflection may begin as sincere practices, yet the ego can gradually turn them into another form of ambition.

The inner voice then says:

> “One day, I will reach the Self.”

At first, this appears to be a noble aspiration. On closer examination, however, the statement contains a subtle error because it imagines the Self as an object to be reached in the future rather than the very awareness in which the present search is already appearing.

## The Geographical Error

The *Upanishads* invite us to examine this error with care. When we imagine that the [Witness Consciousness](/posts/witness-consciousness-and-authentic-action/) must be reached as though it were a distant destination, we treat Consciousness as if it belonged to the same category as places, objects, achievements, and experiences. The Witness Consciousness is not a location within the world. It is the witnessing presence because of which every location, object, thought, and experience becomes known. A person may travel to a holy place, sit in a quiet room, study a sacred text, or observe the mind in meditation, yet all these experiences are already being illumined by awareness. This is why the image of walking toward one’s own feet is useful. The feet do not need to be reached through travel because they are already the ground from which travel begins. In the same way, the Self is not waiting at the end of experience. It is the condition that makes experience possible.

## The Seer

The *Upanishads* begins with a profound inquiry into the source behind ordinary faculties. It asks by whom the mind is directed, by whom breath moves, by whom speech is impelled, and by whom the eye and ear perform their functions. 

Its answer points beyond the visible instruments of experience. The Self is described as the ear of the ear, the mind of the mind, the speech of speech, the breath of breath, and the eye of the eye. This teaching suggests that the Self is not merely another object inside experience. It is the illuminating principle through which experience becomes intelligible. We can see objects, observe thoughts, notice emotions, and become aware of bodily sensations. We can also reflect upon roles, memories, preferences, and personal narratives. Yet the witnessing presence by which these are known cannot be grasped in the same manner as an ordinary object. The eye can see many things, yet it does not see itself directly as one more object in its own field of vision. Similarly, awareness reveals the movements of the mind, but it is not converted into a mental object simply because the mind tries to think about it. This is the subtlety of *Self-inquiry*. The Self is recognized through a refinement of attention, where the seeker gradually becomes less absorbed in the contents of experience and more attentive to the presence by which those contents are known.

## The Pathless Path

A path usually implies movement from one point to another. In ordinary life, this structure is useful because the destination is separate from the traveler. If one wishes to reach a mountain, a temple, a university, or a city, then a path is meaningful because there is geographical distance to be covered. *Self-realization* is of a different order. The central difficulty is not physical distance from the Self, but *misidentification* with what appears within awareness. Attention becomes absorbed in desire, fear, comparison, memory, projection, and social identity. Through this absorption, the body is taken to be the whole Self, the mind is taken to be the whole Self, and the personal story is taken to be the final truth of who we are. The *path* is therefore best understood as a process of careful discernment. It is a gradual loosening of false identification with body, mind, role, memory, and outcome.

The *Upanishads* give us one of the most influential methods of this inquiry through the phrase **Neti Neti**, meaning “not this, not this.” This is not a rejection of life, but a disciplined way of examining false identification. The seeker observes the body, sensations, thoughts, emotions, roles, memories, and achievements, and gradually recognizes that each of them is known within awareness. 

> Since the body is observed, it cannot be the final observer. Since thoughts arise and pass away, they cannot be the unchanging ground of identity. Since roles change according to circumstance, they cannot be the deepest Self. 

Neti Neti allows the seeker to see each layer clearly without denying its practical value. This method does not ask us to abandon the body, mind, or world. It asks us to stop mistaking them for the whole of who we are. By seeing what the Self is not, the seeker becomes prepared to recognize the witnessing awareness that cannot be turned into an object. This is why *Neti Neti* belongs to the pathless path: the Self is not reached by travelling elsewhere, but by releasing the confusion that made it seem absent.

## Learning

The Self is not a future achievement added to the personality. It is the present witnessing awareness in which the personality, the search, the effort, and the longing for realization are all known. The spiritual journey is the patient clarification of what has been overlooked. The seeker begins by looking for the Self as though it were elsewhere, then gradually discovers that the very capacity to seek was already illumined by the Self. Meditation, ethical living, scriptural study, devotion, reflection, and silence all have value because they refine the mind and prepare it for clearer seeing. Their purpose is to remove the confusion that prevents the Self from being recognized. The journey back to who you really are is therefore a journey of understanding. It is a return that does not require distance, a discovery that does not require possession, and a realization that becomes possible when attention ceases to mistake passing appearances for the deepest truth of the Self.

> You cannot walk towards your own feet when you are already standing on them.
