> If being “Sattvic” is not the final goal, then what is?
{: .prompt-tip }

## The Gunas as Bonds

Yesterday, I shared the idea of the [three Gunas](/posts/the-three-gunas/) from the *Bhagavad Gita*: **Tamas**, **Rajas**, and **Sattva**.

One way to think about the Gunas is to see them as bonds. If you are tied up, the material of the rope may change your comfort, but you are still tied.

- **Tamas** is like an iron chain: heavy, dark, and painful.
- **Rajas** is like a copper chain: restless, hot, and active.
- **Sattva** is like a golden chain: beautiful, refined, and bright.

Sattva is clearly more peaceful than Rajas or Tamas. But it is still a chain if we identify with it.

## Prakriti

The Gunas are part of **Prakriti** (Nature).

Nature is always in motion. Even the most Sattvic person will eventually feel the pull of Rajas, perhaps as a new desire, ambition, or restlessness. At another time, they may feel the pull of Tamas through fatigue, dullness, or sleep.

So if I identify completely with Sattva, I will suffer when Sattva inevitably fluctuates.

If I think:

> “I am peaceful.”

Then what happens when peace disappears?

If I think:

> “I am wise.”

Then what happens when confusion arises?

The issue is not Sattva itself. Sattva is valuable because it brings clarity. But the problem begins when Sattva becomes an identity.

## Trigunatita

The deeper goal is to become **Trigunatita** — one who has gone beyond the three Gunas.

At first, this can sound strange.

> Transcend goodness?
{: .prompt-tip }

But the point is not to reject goodness. The point is to stop mistaking any changing state for the Self. To be Trigunatita means returning to the [Witness Consciousness](/posts/witness-consciousness-and-authentic-action/) we reflected on earlier.

- When Tamas appears, you observe the heaviness passing through.
- When Rajas appears, you observe the fire of ambition.
- When Sattva appears, you observe the light of clarity.

You do not deny these states.
You do not suppress them.
You do not cling to them.

You witness them.

## A Different Relationship with the Gunas

A person who has transcended the three Gunas does not stop acting. Instead, their relationship with the Gunas changes.

**In Sattva**, we may seek light and avoid darkness.
**In Trigunatita**, we remain steady whether light or darkness arrives.

This does not mean becoming emotionless or inactive. Transcending the Gunas is like standing on the shore and watching the waves rise and fall.

## Learning

In modern language, we might compare this loosely to a **flow state**. When you are truly in the zone, you aren't thinking, "I am doing a great job" (that would be Sattvic ego). You aren't stressed about the result (Rajas). You aren't bored (Tamas). You have transcended the modes of your personality and become the action itself.

The *Bhagavad Gita* invites us to notice that our moods, ambitions, clarity, dullness, wisdom, and restlessness are all moving within Nature.

But the Witness is deeper than all of them.
