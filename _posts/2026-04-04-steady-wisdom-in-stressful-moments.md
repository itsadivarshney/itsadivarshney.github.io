> When the pressure is on, are your words serving the truth or are they just serving your survival?
{: .prompt-tip }

## Action-Reaction

We’ve all been there.

- A heated argument.  
- A high-stakes meeting.  
- A moment where someone completely misreads our intentions.

Suddenly, the heart rate spikes. The palms sweat. The mind starts racing. And in that moment, the “truth” can start feeling less like a gift and more like a weapon. In high-stress moments, most of us communicate from a place of **Reaction**. When the ego feels threatened, words often become either a shield or a sword.

As a shield, words become defensive.

> “That’s not what I meant.”

> “You’re misunderstanding me.”

> “I didn’t do anything wrong.”

As a sword, words become attacking.

> “You always do this.”

> “This is your fault.”

> “Let me tell you the truth.”

The words may contain some truth but the intention behind them may not be steady.

## Steady Wisdom

In the *Bhagavad Gita*, we are pointed toward the idea of **Steady Wisdom** or **Sthitaprajna**.

A person of steady wisdom does not simply react to stress. They respond to the **Dharma** of the moment. It means their words are not driven by panic, ego, or the need to win. They are able to pause, observe, and act from clarity.

## Dharma

The word Dharma comes from the Sanskrit root **dhṛ**, which means “to uphold” or “to sustain.” 

In the *Bhagavad Gita*, **Dharma** is not just a set of religious rules. Dharma is the right way of being and acting in a given situation. It depends on your nature, your role, your responsibilities, and the specific moment in front of you.

Your Dharma as a friend may be different from your Dharma as a professional.

## The Ocean

The *Bhagavad Gita* gives us a powerful image: the Ocean.

Rivers are constantly pouring into the ocean. Some are calm. Some are forceful. Some arrive gently. Some arrive violently. But the ocean does not expect the rivers to stop.

It does not become complete because the rivers enter.  
It does not become incomplete when they do not.  
It remains full and steady because it is vast.

Similarly, when we act from [Witness Consciousness](/posts/witness-consciousness-and-authentic-action/), we stop trying to get peace from the world. Instead, we begin to notice that the Witness itself is already still. 

## The Gap

The secret to steady wisdom is the gap.

In a high-stress moment, [Witness Consciousness](/posts/witness-consciousness-and-authentic-action/) allows us to notice what is happening inside us.

The heart rate is rising.  
Anger is appearing.  
Defensiveness is forming.  
The need to be right is becoming stronger.

But we do not have to become the anger. We can observe it like weather passing over the ocean. That small gap gives us freedom. In that gap, we can choose a Dharmic response instead of an ego reaction. We stop trying to win the conversation. We start trying to uphold the truth.

## Learning

The *Bhagavad Gita* invites us to act from a steadier place.

- A place where speech is not a shield.  
- A place where speech is not a sword.  
- A place where speech becomes service to Dharma.
