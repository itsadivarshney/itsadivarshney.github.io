> If the Self was never born and will never die, why do gray hairs in the mirror still unsettle us?
{: .prompt-tip }

## Aging

The fear of aging is rarely just about gray hair or lines on the face. The deeper discomfort comes from watching the body change. A body that once felt strong begins to slow down. Recovery takes longer, stamina shifts, and the mirror starts showing time in ways we cannot ignore.

> From the perspective of the *Bhagavad Gita*, the body was never meant to remain fixed. It is part of [Prakriti](/posts/transcending-the-three-gunas/) (material nature) and everything within Prakriti changes.

## The Chariot

The *Upanishad* gives a useful metaphor: human life is like a [chariot](/posts/the-passenger-and-the-driver/). Aging belongs to the chariot but the passenger is not the vehicle. The passenger does not become weaker when the body becomes weaker, it is the Witness of strength, weakness, youth, age, movement, and stillness.

## The Mistake

Our suffering often comes from saying:

> “I am losing my strength.”

A more precise observation would be:

> “The body is losing some of its earlier strength.”

That difference is subtle but powerful. You are aware of the body changing and your awareness of change is itself evidence that you are not merely the change. The [Witness Consciousness](/posts/witness-consciousness-and-authentic-action/) is the one aware that the body once ran easily, and now moves differently.

## Worn-Out Garments

Just as a person discards old clothes and puts on new ones, the embodied self casts off worn-out bodies and receives new ones. This is often applied to death and rebirth. But it can also help us understand the smaller deaths within one lifetime.

> The child-body is gone, the adolescent-body is gone, the younger version of the body is gone, yet something in us has remained present through every garment.

Aging becomes painful when we try to wear an old garment that no longer fits. Peace begins when we honor the body we have now, without mistaking it for the whole of who we are.

## Learning

As our body ages, our Dharma shifts from the External Field to the Internal Field. The later stages of life are designed for the horses to slow down so the Passenger can finally be heard. The inability to sprint allows you the stillness to meditate. The fading of physical noise allows for the rising of spiritual signal. The gray hairs and the slower pace are simply the universe downshifting your vehicle so you can navigate the final, most beautiful turn of the road- “the turn inward”.

**Remember, sometimes the vehicle slows so that the passenger can finally notice the road.**

## Food for Thought

> What part of aging frightens you most?
>
> Is it the body changing, or the fear that you are nothing more than the body?
>
> What would change if you cared for the body deeply, while remembering that the Witness within you remains untouched by age?
