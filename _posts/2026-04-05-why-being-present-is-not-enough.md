> If being present helps us see clearly, then what tells us where to go?
{: .prompt-tip }

## Present

In modern mindfulness circles, we are often told that “being in the present” is the ultimate spiritual goal. But from a philosophical perspective, presence by itself is ethically neutral. You can be completely present and still make a serious moral error. Presence is the lens through which we see the world. But it is not always the map that tells us where to go.

A person can be calm, focused, and aware, yet still choose an action that is not aligned with truth, responsibility, or the needs of the moment.

So the question becomes:

> Once I am present, what should I do?
{: .prompt-tip }

## Dharma and Svadharma

To find that map, the *Bhagavad Gita* introduces us to the idea of **Svadharma**.

[Dharma](/posts/steady-wisdom-in-stressful-moments/) refers to the universal moral imperatives that apply to everyone regardless of their social standing or stage of life. They include virtues such as satya (truth), ahimsa (non-violence), and asteya (non-stealing). 

**Svadharma** comes from:
- **sva**: one’s own
- **dharma**: duty
So, **Svadharma** means one’s own Dharma.

It is the localized and personal expression of Dharma.
> For a student, Svadharma may be to learn sincerely. For a parent, it may be to nurture and guide. For a leader, it may be to protect the collective. For a friend, it may be to listen honestly. For a professional, it may be to act with competence and responsibility.

Svadharma reminds us that right action is not always generic. It depends on who we are, where we stand, and what the moment requires from us.

To better understand Svadharma, let's look at it from two dimensions:
- Nature
- Role and Position

### Dimension 1: Nature

The first dimension of Svadharma is our nature.

The *Bhagavad Gita* teaches that it is better to perform one’s own Dharma imperfectly than to perform another person’s Dharma perfectly. It asks us to stop imitating someone else’s version of “goodness” and instead ask:

> What is mine to do?
{: .prompt-tip }

Are we acting from our own nature, or are we performing a version of goodness that society expects from us?

- A surgeon should not try to act like a soldier.
- A leader should not act like a bystander.
- A teacher should not act like a performer.
- A parent should not act like a distant observer.

When we abandon our own nature and imitate another person’s path, something becomes misaligned. The action may look impressive from the outside, but inwardly it may not be true.

### Dimension 2: Role and Position

The second dimension of Svadharma is role and position.

We are never just abstract human beings floating outside context. At any given moment, we may be:

- a parent
- a child
- a friend
- a professional
- a leader
- a student

Each role carries a different responsibility. The same action may be appropriate in one role and inappropriate in another.

For example, a leader’s Dharma in a crisis may require speaking a difficult truth to protect the collective. If that leader chooses “kindness” only because they want to feel peaceful, liked, or non-confrontational, then they may not actually be acting from Dharma. They may be protecting their own comfort.

## Learning

Presence shows us what is happening and Svadharma shows us what the moment requires. True authenticity is not merely the expression of our feelings.

When faced with a difficult choice, Svadharma asks:

> Given my specific nature and my current position in this relationship, organization, or situation, what action is required to uphold the integrity of the whole?
{: .prompt-tip }

That question is not always comfortable. Sometimes doing the right thing feels heavy because it conflicts with the ego. But it may also feel light because it aligns with truth.

## Food for Thought

> Think of a difficult decision you are facing.
>
> If you removed your desire to be liked, comfortable, or seen as good, and looked only at your nature and your position, does the right move become clearer?
>
> Is it possible that doing the right thing feels heavy because it challenges your ego, but light because it aligns with your truth?
{: .prompt-tip }
