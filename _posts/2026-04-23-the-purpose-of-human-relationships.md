> Have you ever wondered what the goal of a successful relationship is?
{: .prompt-tip }

## The Myth of Two Halves

The modern romantic ideal often tells us that **two halves** must find each other to become **one whole**. It sounds beautiful but philosophically it can become dangerous. If I believe I am incomplete then I may begin searching for someone else to complete me. Further, if I believe I am only half then I may place the weight of my entire existence on another person’s shoulders. That is not love but dependency dressed as romance.

A relationship built on this premise:

> “You must become the missing part of me.”

## Yajnavalkya and Maitreyi

To understand how two people can exist together without losing themselves, we can turn to the dialogue between the sage **Yajnavalkya** and his wife **Maitreyi** in the *Brihadaranyaka Upanishad*. Maitreyi asks a profound question about immortality as she wants to know what truly lasts. Yajnavalkya then offers one of the most powerful teachings on love:

> It is not for the sake of the husband that the husband is dear but for the sake of the [Self](/posts/the-power-of-stepping-back-into-authentic-action/)(Witness Consciousness) that the husband is dear. It is not for the sake of the wife that the wife is dear but for the sake of the Self that the wife is dear.
{: .prompt-tip }

The teaching continues beyond husband and wife. Everything we hold dear is dear because at the deepest level it reflects the Self.

## The Self in the Other

This means that relationships are deeper than personality. When we truly love someone, perhaps we are catching a glimpse of the Infinite through the window of that person. Their presence becomes a mirror. The goal of a relationship, then, is not to merge two egos into one blurry identity but the recognition that both individuals are already connected at the level of the Self. You do not become one by losing yourself in another person. You remember the deeper unity that was always there.

## Purnamadah Purnamidam

One of my favorite teachings from the *Upanishad* is:

> “Purnamadah Purnamidam”
>
> “That is whole; this is whole. From wholeness, wholeness comes. When wholeness is taken from wholeness, wholeness alone remains.”
{: .prompt-tip }

This is a profound statement of completeness. It challenges the idea that we are fragments searching for completion outside ourselves. If you approach a partner as a “half,” you are operating from **Abhava**, a sense of lack. You may begin demanding that the other person fill your loneliness, insecurity, emptiness, or need for status. But the teaching of wholeness says something different. **You are already Purna (whole) at the level of the Self**.

## Two Lamps

Another way to look at relationships is that you are not a 0.5 searching for another 0.5. You are a 1.0 standing next to another 1.0. When two whole beings come together, they are like two lamps in the same room. The light of one lamp does not disappear into the other. The light does not need to consume or possess. The room simply becomes brighter. This is a very different model of relationship.

Instead of saying:

> “Become the source of my meaning.”

It says:

> “Let us help each other remember the meaning already present within.”

## The Burden of Being Everything

In today’s world, relationships often fail because we expect one person to become everything.

We expect one person to be:

- lover
- best friend
- therapist
- spiritual guide
- emotional regulator
- source of identity
- proof of our worth
- cure for loneliness
- witness to every part of our life

This is too much weight for one human being.

When we place the burden of our entire existence on another person, love becomes heavy.

The relationship becomes less about meeting the other and more about using the other to stabilize ourselves.

This is where attachment begins to replace love.

## Loving From Wholeness

When we recognize our own wholeness, we release the other person from the impossible job of fixing us. This does not mean we become emotionally distant. It means we become cleaner in our love. We can still need support, still seek affection, and still be vulnerable, but we no longer make another person responsible for our fundamental sense of being. That responsibility belongs to our relationship with the Self.

## Learning

The goal of a successful relationship is not fusion. It is two people becoming more truthful, more whole, and more awake in each other’s presence.

A relationship rooted in wholeness says:

> “Because I am whole, I can love you without making you responsible for my existence.”

## Food for Thought

> In your closest relationships, are you loving the other person as they are, or are you asking them to repair your sense of incompleteness?
>
> What would change if you approached love not as two halves trying to become one, but as two whole beings learning how to illuminate the same room?
