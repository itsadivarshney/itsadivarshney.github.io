> What if chanting OM is not simply repeating a sacred sound but a way of returning attention to the Self that silently witnesses all experience?

## OM ॐ

Most of us have heard **OM** chanted at the beginning or end of a prayer, meditation, or yoga practice. It is easy to treat it as a spiritual sound, something calming, something traditional, something sacred, but perhaps not fully understood.

In the *Upanishad*, OM is not just a sound, it is a doorway into understanding our [Witness Consciousness](/posts/witness-consciousness-and-authentic-action/). OM is used as a symbol for the whole of experience and the deeper Self that witnesses experience. It is the primordial sound of the universe, ultimate
reality, and consciousness. So when we chant OM, the goal is not merely to produce a vibration rather to turn inward, to observe, to become still, and to recognize the awareness behind the changing states of the mind.

## The Mandukya Upanishad

The *Mandukya Upanishad* is one of the shortest yet most profound of the *Upanishads*. Its entire focus is the nature of the Self or *Witness Consciousness* through the symbol of OM.

It explains that OM can be understood through three sounds:

- **A**
- **U**
- **M**

And then through the **silence** that follows the sound.

## A: The Waking State

The sound **A** represents the waking state. This is the state in which we are outward-facing where we interact with the world through the senses. 

> When we chant the **A** sound, we can reflect on the part of us that is engaged with the outer world.

## U: The Dream State

The sound **U** represents the dream state. In dreams, the outer world is no longer directly present. The mind creates its own world, it produces people, places, emotions, fears, desires, and stories. The mind can create an entire reality and then become absorbed in it. This is not only true during sleep. Even while awake, we often live inside mental projections. We imagine futures, we replay conversations, we build stories about ourselves and others.

> When we chant the **U** sound, we can reflect on the inner world of thought, memory, imagination, and emotion.

## M: Deep Sleep

The sound **M** represents deep sleep. In deep sleep, there is no active outer world, there is no dream world, and there is no clear sense of “I am this person doing this thing.” And yet, when we wake up, we say:

> “I slept well.”

This suggests that even when ordinary mental activity becomes quiet, some deeper continuity remains. Deep sleep points us toward a state where the usual ego-noise is absent.

> When we chant the **M** sound, we can reflect on the quieting of the mind.

## The Silence After OM

The most important part of OM may be the **silence** after the sound. After **A**, **U**, and **M**, there is stillness. The *Mandukya Upanishad* points to this silence as **Turiya**, the fourth. Turiya is not simply another ordinary state like waking, dreaming, or deep sleep. It is the awareness in which all states appear. It is the *Witness Consciousness* which is present in waking, dreaming, and deep sleep.

## OM and Bhagavad Gita

The *Bhagavad Gita* mentions OM as a sacred syllable connected with Brahman, the Absolute. Krishna teaches that one who chants OM while remembering the Supreme, moves toward the highest goal. This is important because it reminds us that chanting is not meant to be empty sound. It is in remembering what is deepest while living life.

## Learning

Chanting OM is not about performing spirituality. It is a practice of returning. It begins as sound and ends in silence. And in that silence we may begin to recognize who we really are.

## Food for Thought

> The next time you chant OM, pause after the sound fades.
>
> What remains in that silence?
