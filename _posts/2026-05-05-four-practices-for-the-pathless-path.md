> If the search for Moksha was partly a game of hide-and-seek with yourself then would you still be anxious about finding the truth?
{: .prompt-tip }

## The Hide-and-Seek

In the previous [reflection](/posts/the-pathless-path-to-the-self/), we explored the idea of the pathless path where the Witness is not waiting at the end of a spiritual marathon but is already the awareness in which the search itself appears.

So why do we still feel lost?

Because attention keeps identifying with what it observes. We mistake the body for “me.” We mistake thoughts for “me.” We mistake roles for “me.” We mistake outcomes for “me.” The spiritual task is to stop hiding from it through misidentification.

This reflection shares four practical ways to do that.

## 1. Practice the Subject-Object Audit

Throughout the day, notice what is being observed.

When the thought appears:

> “I am stressed.”

Pause and reframe it:

> “Stress is being observed in awareness.”

This is not wordplay. It is discrimination. If stress can be observed then stress is an object of awareness. It is not the deepest Subject. The same applies to the body. The body is seen, sensations are seen, aging is seen, fatigue is seen, pleasure is seen, and pain is also seen. Therefore, the body is experienced within awareness. The same applies to thoughts. Therefore, thoughts are not also the final Self.

The principle is simple:

> You are not what you can observe.
{: .prompt-tip }

You are the awareness in which observation happens.

## 2. See Roles as Roles

We also tend to identify with roles: parent, student, professional, partner, friend, leader, seeker. These roles matter, they are part of Dharma and they carry responsibilities. But they are not the [Atman][Witness Consciousness](/posts/witness-consciousness-and-authentic-action/). They are functions of the persona. A role can be performed sincerely without being mistaken for the Self. You can be a professional without your worth depending on performance. The game becomes lighter when you remember:

> “This role is mine to perform but it is not the whole of who I am.”

Play the role well but do not let the scoreboard define your being.

## 3. Act Fully, Release the Result

The *Bhagavad Gita* teaches action without attachment to results. Do your task with full intensity because it is your Dharma and do not make the outcome the proof of your existence. When the thought “I did this” becomes too strong, anxiety follows because then the next thought is:

> “What if I fail?”

The false doer creates the false burden. The *Bhagavad Gita* reminds us that actions unfold through [Prakriti](/posts/transcending-the-three-gunas/) and the [Gunas](/posts/the-three-gunas/), while ego mistakenly thinks, “I am the doer.” Your responsibility is effort, clarity, and alignment. The result belongs to a wider field.

## 4. Sit in Silence

Set aside time for silence. Simply sit, let the body be still, let the mind reveal its movement, let thoughts arise and pass and let the search soften. **If the search is a game of hide-and-seek, silence is the moment you stop running**. The seeker becomes quiet and in that quietness, it becomes easier to notice that the Sought was never elsewhere. The Witness was present before the practice.

## Learning

The "You" that feels bound is merely a thought-pattern within the mind. Once this is seen, you continue to live, to work, and to love, not to get happy, but from a state of happiness.
