> If the search for Moksha was partly a game of hide-and-seek with yourself then would you still be anxious about finding the truth?
{: .prompt-tip }

## The Hide-and-Seek

In the previous [reflection](/posts/the-journey-back-to-who-you-really-are/), we explored the idea of the pathless path, where the Witness is not waiting at the end of a spiritual marathon, but is already the awareness within which the entire search appears. This raises an important question: if the Self is not truly absent then why do we still feel lost?

The answer lies in the movement of attention and identification. Attention repeatedly attaches itself to what it observes, and through habit, memory, conditioning, and ego-sense, we begin to mistake the observed for the observer. We identify with the body and say, *I am this form.* We identify with thoughts and say, *I am this mental movement.* We identify with roles and say, *I am this social function.* We identify with outcomes and say, *I am successful,* or *I am a failure.* The spiritual task, therefore, is not to manufacture the Self as though it were missing, but to recognize the ways in which we hide from it through misidentification. The Witness is not absent; it is overlooked because attention is absorbed in body, mind, role, and result. This reflection offers four practical habits for weakening that false identification and returning, again and again, to the recognition of [Witness Consciousness](/posts/the-power-of-stepping-back-into-authentic-action/).

## 1. Practice the Subject-Object Audit

Throughout the day, gently notice what is being observed.

When the thought appears:

> “I am stressed.”

Pause and reframe it with greater precision:

> “Stress is being observed in awareness.”

This practice is not a linguistic trick or a motivational exercise. It is a form of discrimination, known in Vedantic inquiry as the capacity to distinguish the Seer from the seen, the Subject from the object, and the witnessing awareness from the contents that arise within it. If stress can be observed, then stress is an object appearing in awareness. It may be intense, and it may deserve care, but it is still known by something deeper than itself. The same principle applies to the body. The body is seen, sensations are known, aging is noticed, hunger is experienced, fatigue is registered, pleasure is recognized, and pain is also observed. Since all these are objects of experience, they cannot be the final Subject.

The principle is simple, but its implications are profound:

> You are not what you can observe.

You are the awareness within which observation happens. This does not mean dismissing the body, ignoring emotions, or pretending that suffering is unreal at the practical level. Rather, it means learning to relate to experience with clarity, so that temporary conditions are not mistaken for the deepest identity.

## 2. See Roles as Roles

We also tend to identify strongly with roles: parent, student, professional, partner, friend, leader, seeker, helper, achiever, or guide. These roles are meaningful and many of them are connected to Dharma because they carry duties, responsibilities, and ethical obligations. A person should not use spirituality as an excuse to neglect them. At the same time, a role remains a role. It is a function expressed through the body-mind-personality, shaped by circumstance, relationship, and responsibility. It may be sincere, noble, and necessary, yet it is still not the Witness Consciousness that illumines every role without being confined to any one of them. A role can be performed with full integrity without being mistaken for the whole Self. You can be a professional without allowing professional performance to become the measure of your worth. You can be a friend without making another person’s approval the foundation of your identity. You can be a seeker without turning spirituality itself into another egoic label.

The game of life becomes lighter when you remember:

> “This role is mine to perform but it is not the whole of who I am.”

This recognition does not make action careless; in fact, it can make action more authentic. When the role is no longer burdened with the impossible task of proving your existence, you can perform it with greater steadiness, humility, and freedom.

## 3. Act Fully, Release the Result

The *Bhagavad Gita* teaches a profound discipline of action without attachment to results - [Authentic Action](/posts/the-power-of-stepping-back-into-authentic-action/). Krishna’s instruction is not a call to passivity, indifference, or weak effort. Rather, it is a call to act with full commitment while refusing to make the outcome the final proof of one’s identity. This teaching is especially relevant because the ego often turns action into self-definition. When the thought *I alone did this* becomes too strong, the mind quickly becomes anxious because the next movement is fear: *What if I fail?* In this way, the false sense of isolated doership creates a burden that the individual mind cannot easily carry.

> “What if I fail?”

The *Bhagavad Gita* teaches that actions unfold through [Prakriti](/posts/rising-above-the-noise-of-daily-life/), through nature, and through the movement of the [Gunas](/posts/moving-beyond-the-traps-of-the-ego/), while the ego, under delusion, thinks, *I am the doer.* This does not remove personal responsibility at the practical level, but it places that responsibility within a wider field of causes, conditions, tendencies, duties, and consequences. Your responsibility is effort, clarity, discipline, and alignment with Dharma. The result, however, belongs to a larger order. Many forces shape an outcome: your intention, your preparation, the actions of others, timing, environment, past tendencies, collective conditions, and factors beyond your control. To act authentically is therefore to give yourself fully to the action while gradually releasing the psychological demand that the result must validate your existence. You are responsible for the sincerity of the offering, but you are not the sole owner of the universe in which the offering bears fruit.

## 4. Sit in Silence

Set aside time for silence. Sit quietly, allow the body to become still and observe how thoughts arise, linger, change, and pass away within awareness. Silence is powerful because it exposes the mechanics of identification. In ordinary activity, the mind moves quickly from one object to another and because the movement is continuous, we rarely notice the awareness in which the movement occurs. When silence is introduced, the mind initially becomes more visible. Desires, fears, memories, plans, and unfinished emotions may rise to the surface, not because silence creates them, but because silence allows them to be seen.

If the spiritual search is partly a game of hide-and-seek, then silence is the moment when we stop running long enough to notice where we have been hiding. The seeker becomes quieter, and in that quietness, it becomes easier to recognize that the Sought was never elsewhere. The Witness was present before the practice began. It is present during the practice. It remains present after the practice ends. This is why silence should not be treated merely as a technique for relaxation, although it may also bring calmness. Its deeper value lies in helping attention withdraw from constant identification with mental movement and return to the witnessing presence that knows the movement.

## Learning

Inner growth begins with simple, repeated acts of discernment: seeing the body as observed, seeing thoughts as observed, seeing roles as performed, and seeing outcomes as part of a wider field rather than as the final measure of the Self.
