> If “speaking the truth” and “doing the right thing” are tools, then what are we building with them?
{: .prompt-tip }

## The Three Gunas

To elaborate on this topic, let me share the idea of the **three Gunas** from the *Bhagavad Gita*.

The Gunas are the qualities of nature. They make up everything in the universe, including our personalities, tendencies, moods, and actions. Think of them as the operating system (OS) of the mind.

### Tamas

**Tamas** represents inertia, darkness, heaviness, and confusion.

This is the state where we may feel lazy, dull, disconnected, or indifferent.

It sounds like:

> “I don’t care.”

Or:

> “It’s too hard.”

When Tamas is driving us, we may avoid action, postpone responsibility, or remain stuck even when we know something needs to change.

### Rajas

**Rajas** represents passion, activity, desire, and restlessness.

This is the state of ambition, striving, comparison, and constant movement.

It sounds like:

> “I want this.”

Or:

> “I must win.”

Rajas can be useful because it gets us moving. But when it dominates, we become attached to achievement, recognition, results, and control.

### Sattva

**Sattva** represents purity, balance, clarity, and wisdom.

This is the state where we feel calm, thoughtful, and aligned. It is usually where “speaking the truth” and “doing the right thing” live.

Sattva is the noun form, while **Sattvic** is the adjective. So, a Sattvic action, thought, food, or person is infused with the quality of Sattva.

## The Golden Ego

Most of us may think that the ultimate goal of life is to become Sattvic.

And that makes sense.

When we are in a Sattvic state, we may feel:

> “I am peaceful.”

> “I am wise.”

> “I am doing the right thing.”

But there is a subtle trap here. Even Sattva can create pride. This is what we might call the **Golden Ego**.

Unlike ordinary ego, the golden ego does not necessarily say:

> “I am better because I am powerful.”

Instead, it says:

> “I am better because I am peaceful.”

Or:

> “I am better because I am right.”

Or:

> “I am better because I am spiritual.”

This is harder to notice because it wears the clothing of goodness.

## Learning

If our peace depends on the world being quiet, people being kind, or circumstances going our way, then our peace is still dependent on external conditions.

It may be refined, but it is still fragile.

We may still be looking for a result:

- peace
- praise
- validation
- moral superiority
- the feeling of being “right”

This is why the *Bhagavad Gita’s* teaching goes deeper than simply becoming good, calm, or wise.

Even goodness can become an identity.
Even wisdom can become pride.
Even doing the right thing can become another way for the ego to feel special.

That does not mean Sattva is bad. Sattva is necessary. It brings clarity and balance. But perhaps Sattva is not the final destination. Perhaps it is a bridge. Today’s post is intended to be a cliffhanger. More clarity to come in the next post.
