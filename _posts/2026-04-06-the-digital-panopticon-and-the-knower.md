> Why does it sometimes feel that the more we are seen by the world, the more we are disappearing?
{: .prompt-tip }

## The Panopticon

To explore this, let’s look at Jeremy Bentham’s **Panopticon**. The Panopticon was a circular prison design where a single guard could observe every inmate from a central tower. The prisoners, however, could not know whether they were being watched at any particular moment. That uncertainty was the psychological brilliance of the design.

> The prisoner did not need to be watched all the time. They only needed to believe that they might be watched.

Over time, the prisoner internalized the guard. They began to regulate their own behaviour, movements, and expressions according to the expected norm. In a way, they became both the inmate and the architect of their own surveillance.

## The Digital Panopticon

Today, we have built a different kind of Panopticon.

> Not circular. Not made of stone. Not guarded by a single inspector. But digital.

On social media, we live under the constant and collective gaze of the “Other". We are not always being watched by one person, but we are always potentially visible to many.

So we begin to curate.

> We curate our achievements. We curate our aesthetics. We curate our opinions. We curate our happiness. We curate even our vulnerability.

Slowly, the performance can swallow the performer. The version of ourselves that is successful, pleasing, thoughtful, attractive, intelligent, or morally correct becomes so important that we forget to ask:

> Who is the one trying to be seen?
{: .prompt-tip }

The *Bhagavad Gita* addresses this existential fragmentation through the distinction between **Kshetra** and **Kshetrajna**.

## Kshetra

**Kshetra** means the **Field**.

It refers to the object of experience — everything that can be observed.

This includes:
- body
- ego
- reputation
- social identity
- professional status
- digital profile

In modern terms, Kshetra includes the entire visible and measurable version of who we appear to be. It is everything that can be seen, judged, praised, criticized, improved, branded, or compared.

## Kshetrajna

**Kshetrajna** means the **Knower of the Field**.

This is the observing consciousness. It is the subject, not the object. It is the silent awareness that knows the body, thoughts, emotions, reputation, and social identity as things appearing in experience.

> The Kshetrajna is not your profile. It is the one aware of the profile. It is not your reputation. It is the one aware of the reputation. It is not your anxiety about being seen. It is the one that can observe the anxiety.

This connects directly with [Witness Consciousness](/posts/witness-consciousness-and-authentic-action/).

## Ahankara

Our modern suffering often comes from *mistaking the Field for the Knower*. We begin to believe:

> “I am my reputation.”

> “I am my likes.”

> “I am my role.”

> “I am my image.”

> “I am how others perceive me.”

But all of these belong to the Field. They are visible, changeable, and dependent on external conditions. This confusion is driven by **Ahankara** (ego).

Ahankara is the “I-maker” — the psychological function that says:

> “This is me.”

> “This is mine.”

> “This is my success.”

> “This is my image.”

> “This is my failure.”

> “This is how I must be seen.”

When we operate from Ahankara, we become fragile because our identity depends on reflection. We feel real only when someone else confirms us. We feel successful only when someone else notices us. We feel meaningful only when the digital audience responds.

In that state, we are like ghosts in our own lives, waiting to become visible in someone else’s eyes.

## Learning

The *Bhagavad Gita* guides us to stop confusing the changing field of experience with the unchanging awareness that knows it. A way out is to practice [Witness Consciousness](/posts/witness-consciousness-and-authentic-action/).

This is the disciplined habit of emotionally detaching from the performance and returning to the position of the observer. 

> If you can see your anxiety about your reputation, then you are not the anxiety. You are the one observing it. If you can observe your desire for validation, then you are not the desire. You are the Knower of the desire.

## Food for Thought

> For the next 48 hours, try performing one act of high personal significance. It could be a hard task, a genuine kindness, or a meaningful act of discipline.
>
> Then, strictly forbid yourself from sharing it.
>
> If nobody else sees it, does the act feel less real to you? If so, who are you truly living for: the Knower, or the Guard in the Panopticon?
