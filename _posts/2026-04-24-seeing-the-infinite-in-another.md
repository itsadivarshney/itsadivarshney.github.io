> What does it mean to catch a glimpse of the Infinite through the window of another person?
{: .prompt-tip }

## The Infinite

In the previous [reading](/posts/goal-of-relationships/), we reflected on relationships and wholeness. But one phrase deserves a deeper pause:

> “Catching a glimpse of the Infinite through the window of another person.”

What does that actually mean? To understand this, we first need to ask: what is the Infinite? From the *Upanishad*, the Infinite is not merely a very large space, it is **Brahman** — fullness, completeness, and the undivided ground of reality. It is **Purna** — whole, lacking nothing. In our daily life, however, we usually see only the finite. We see a person’s height, face, mood, profession, social status, bank account, personality, opinions, and history. A glimpse of the Infinite is the moment when the mask briefly slips.

## The Two Birds

To understand this, we can turn to one of the most beautiful images from the *Upanishad*: the story of the two birds.

Two birds sit on the same tree. They are inseparable companions. One bird eats the fruits of the tree. The other bird does not eat, it simply watches.

The first bird is the **Eater**. It represents the individual self as experienced through personality, ego, desire, memory, and reaction. This bird is fully engaged in the world. It enjoys the sweet fruit and suffers from the bitter fruit. It wants more of what feels good and wants less of what hurts. This bird reflects the finite self — the part shaped by experience, preference, attachment, and aversion.

The second bird is the **Watcher**. It represents the deeper Self — **Atman**, the [Witness Consciousness](/posts/witness-consciousness-and-authentic-action/). The Watcher is the silent awareness in which joy, sorrow, attraction, fear, desire, and memory all appear. This is the Infinite side of us.

## Relationship as Two Eaters

Most of the time, relationships happen between two Eaters. One personality meets another personality. One set of needs meets another set of needs. This is not wrong. But if love stays only at this level it becomes fragile because the fruits keep changing.

## The Glimpse of Infinite

A glimpse of the Infinite happens when for a moment the Eater in the other person becomes quiet. And through their eyes, their silence, or their unguarded presence, you sense something deeper. This is why there are moments in love that feel strangely sacred. Not dramatic but still. *For example, you may look at someone you love while they are sleeping, or resting, or completely unperformed*.

> They are not trying to impress you. They are not being useful to you. They are not giving you anything. And yet, affection arises. Not because of what they are doing. Simply because they are.

That is the glimpse.

## Purnamadah Purnamidam

This brings us back to the Upanishadic mantra:

> “Purnamadah Purnamidam”
>
> “That is whole; this is whole. From wholeness, wholeness comes. When wholeness is taken from wholeness, wholeness alone remains.”
{: .prompt-tip }

This teaching reminds us that the deeper reality within another person is not something we complete. It is something we recognize. A healthy relationship does not begin with two halves trying to become one. It begins with two expressions of wholeness learning how to honor each other. Your partner is not a half who needs you to complete them and you are not a half waiting to be completed. You are each Whole at the level of the Self.

## Learning

To see the Infinite in another person is to remember that beneath the changing personality, there is a deeper awareness that cannot be reduced to mood, behavior, success, failure, beauty, or usefulness. This is the highest form of love because it refuses to reduce the other person to what they provide.

## Food for Thought

> Have you ever looked at someone you love in a moment when they were completely unperformed and felt affection that had nothing to do with what they were doing for you?
>
> What if that affection was not merely sentiment, but a glimpse of the deeper Self shining through the finite person?
