## OM ॐ

Most of us have heard **OM** chanted at the beginning or end of a prayer, meditation, or yoga practice. It may appear familiar because it belongs to the soundscape of spiritual life. In the *Upanishads*, OM is treated with great seriousness because it serves as a sacred symbol through which the seeker contemplates the nature of reality and the Self. It gathers the mind and turns attention inward.

In the *Upanishads*, OM is not just a sound, it is a doorway into understanding our [Witness Consciousness](/posts/the-power-of-stepping-back-into-authentic-action/). It presents OM as a symbol for the whole field of experience and the deeper Self that knows that experience. Through OM, the seeker is invited to examine waking, dreaming, deep sleep, and the awareness that remains present through all changing states. When we chant OM with understanding, the purpose is not simply to produce a pleasant vibration. The chant becomes a disciplined movement of attention. Sound gathers the mind, sustained awareness refines the mind, and the silence that follows the sound invites the mind to rest in the presence that witnesses experience.

## The Mandukya Upanishad

The *Mandukya Upanishad* is one of the shortest yet most profound of the *Upanishads*. Its entire focus is the nature of the Self or *Witness Consciousness* through the symbol of OM.

It explains that OM can be understood through three sounds:

- **A**
- **U**
- **M**

And then through the **silence** that follows the sound.

This structure is philosophically significant as it maps ordinary experience onto a sacred syllable. The sounds **A**, **U**, and **M** correspond to waking, dreaming, and deep sleep. The silence after OM points toward **Turiya**, the fourth, which is not an ordinary state like the other three. Turiya refers to the witnessing awareness because of which all states are known.

### A: The Waking State

The sound **A** represents the waking state, known as *Jagrat*. This is the state in which consciousness is directed outward through the senses and engages with the shared world of action, responsibility, relationship, and practical experience. In the waking state, we usually take the external world to be the primary field of reality. We work, speak, plan, respond, and act as though the visible world is the whole of experience. The *Upanishads* teaching invites us to notice that even this outward-facing state is known within awareness.

> When we chant the **A** sound, we can reflect on the part of us that is engaged with the outer world.

### U: The Dream State

The sound **U** represents the dream state, known as *Svapna*. In dreams, the external world is no longer directly present yet the mind still creates a complete field of experience. The dream has images, movement, relationships, fear, desire, and meaning while it is being experienced. This state reveals the creative power of the mind. During a dream, the mind can generate an entire world and then become absorbed in that world. This insight is valuable even for waking life because much of our ordinary experience is shaped by inner interpretation. We carry mental stories, projected fears, imagined futures, and remembered conversations into the present moment.

> When we chant the **U** sound, we can reflect on the inner world of thought, memory, imagination, and emotion.

### M: Deep Sleep

The sound **M** represents deep sleep, known as *Sushupti*. In deep sleep, the outward world is absent from active experience, and the dream world has also become quiet. The ordinary sense of personal identity is no longer functioning in the way it does during waking and dreaming. Yet when we wake, we may say:

> “I slept well.”

This suggests that even when ordinary mental activity becomes quiet, some deeper continuity remains. Deep sleep points us toward a state where the usual movements of thought and ego have become quiet.

> When we chant the **M** sound, we can reflect on the quieting of mental activity and the temporary withdrawal of personal identity.

Deep sleep should not be confused with Self-realization. It is still a state that comes and goes. Its contemplative value lies in showing that the Self cannot be reduced to the restless activity of the waking mind.

### The Silence After OM

The silence after **A-U-M** is central to the contemplative meaning of OM. After the sound fades, there remains a stillness that cannot be captured by another audible syllable. The *Upanishads* uses this silence to point toward **Turiya**, the fourth state of consciousness. Turiya is not simply another experience that appears after waking, dreaming, and deep sleep. It is the witnessing awareness through which all three states are known. Waking appears and disappears. Dreaming appears and disappears. Deep sleep appears and disappears. Turiya points to the awareness that is not exhausted by any one of these states. This is why the silence after OM is important. The sound prepares the mind and the silence invites the mind to recognize the witnessing presence that was already there before the chant began. The practice therefore moves from sound to stillness and from stillness toward Self-recognition.

## OM and Bhagavad Gita

In the *Bhagavad Gita*, Krishna teaches that one who departs from the body while chanting OM and remembering the Supreme attains the highest goal. This teaching is important because it connects sound with remembrance. OM is not presented as an empty sound that works mechanically apart from consciousness. Its spiritual force is joined with remembrance, devotion, and inward absorption in the Supreme. For daily practice, this means that chanting OM should be approached with attention rather than mere repetition. The sound becomes meaningful when the mind is gathered, the heart is oriented towards the Divine, and the practitioner remains attentive to the silence into which the sound dissolves.

## Learning

Chanting OM is a practice of return. It begins with sound, gathers the mind, and leads attention toward silence. In that silence, the seeker may begin to notice the awareness that has been present through waking, dreaming, deep sleep, and every movement of the mind. OM teaches the mind to move from outward activity to inward stillness and from inward stillness toward the witnessing awareness that silently illumines all experience.
