> Is there a practical way to stop being so fixated on the material world?
{: .prompt-tip }

## Simple Living

Yes. One practical way is to **simplify life** because every unnecessary possession can become another place where the mind gets stuck. When we own a thousand things, the mind often lives in a thousand locations. Simplicity gives the mind fewer hooks. It reclaims attention which is the doorway through which we turn inward.

## Enjoy Through Renunciation

The *Upanishad* gives a powerful instruction:

> Enjoy through renunciation.
{: .prompt-tip }

This sounds contradictory at first. How can you enjoy something by renouncing it? The point is to reject possessiveness, to use what is needed, appreciate what is present, and to not build your identity on ownership.

> A simple person is not owned by what they own.

## The Lotus Leaf

The *Bhagavad Gita* gives us the image of lotus leaf. The lotus leaf lives in water, but water does not cling to it. In the same way, Krishna teaches that one who performs action without attachment remains untouched, like a lotus leaf untouched by water. You do not need to run away from the pond, you need to stop becoming soaked by it.

## Learning

Simple living is clarity. It is the decision to stop scattering consciousness across unnecessary desires. You move from being a *Consumer* of the world to being a *Witness* of the world. This lightness of being is the essential prerequisite for [Moksha](/posts/beyond-heaven-and-hell/). You cannot fly into the Infinite if you are carrying the luggage of the entire material world on your back.

Moksha (Liberation) is not a destination you reach after you die; it is the freedom you feel right now when you realize that there is nothing in this material world that can add to you and nothing that can be taken away from you.

## Food for Thought

> What is one thing you own, maintain, or chase that quietly owns part of your attention?
>
> If you used it without building identity around it, would your relationship with it become lighter?
