> Is prayer a way to get what you want or a way to become who you were meant to be?
{: .prompt-tip }

## Prayer

If we assume the presence of a higher intelligence as described in the *Bhagavad Gita*, then a shopping-list style of prayer begins to feel incomplete. If [Ishvara](/posts/life-as-a-symphony/) is all-knowing and perfectly aware of what lives in the heart then why would we need to inform the Infinite of our finite needs?

## Arjuna and Krishna

In the *Bhagavad Gita*, the relationship between Krishna (the Divine) and Arjuna (the seeking soul) is not built on blind instruction but as a dialogue. Krishna already knows the larger movement of the battle, but He still allows Arjuna to ask, struggle, doubt, collapse, question, and slowly awaken. The Divine does not merely impose clarity on Arjuna. Arjuna has to become ready to receive it. In this sense, prayer is not only a request. **It is participation in a *dialogue* between the confused self and the deeper intelligence that can guide it**.

## Buddhi Yoga

This is where prayer can become **Buddhi Yoga**. Buddhi Yoga is the yoga of clarified intelligence. When we pray sincerely, we are bringing the restless [Manas](/posts/intuition-ego-or-witness/), the emotional mind, into the presence of a higher order of intelligence.

The prayer may begin as:

> “Please give me what I want.”

But if it deepens, it becomes:

> “Help me understand what is right.”
{: .prompt-tip }

That is a major shift. The first prayer tries to bend reality toward desire while the second prayer tries to align desire with truth.

## Sakama Bhakti

The *Bhagavad Gita* acknowledges that people approach the Divine from different starting points.

> Some approach in distress. Some seek knowledge. Some seek material gain. Some seek the Divine through wisdom.

When devotion is driven mainly by personal gain, we might call it **Sakama Bhakti** — devotion with desire or agenda.

Here, prayer says:

> “Please give me this result.”

> “Please remove this discomfort.”

> “Please make this person change.”

> “Please make my path easier.”

This kind of prayer remains centered around **Ahankara**, the ego-sense that believes a particular external result is the key to happiness.

## Nishkama Bhakti

Over time, prayer can mature into **Nishkama Bhakti** — devotion without the motive of personal gain. Here, the prayer changes.

It is no longer primarily:

> “Give me what I want.”

It becomes:

> “Make me capable of receiving what is true.”

It becomes:

> “Let me act according to Dharma.”

This is where prayer stops being a transaction and becomes transformation. The focus moves from changing the world to purifying the self that meets the world.

## Sharanagati

The deeper movement of prayer is **Sharanagati** — surrender. Surrender does not mean giving up responsibility rather it means releasing the ego’s claim that it knows, controls, and owns everything.

## Learning

Prayer is not a transaction designed to change the external world but a process designed to align the internal world. This understanding transforms prayer into a daily act of re-tuning and practicing [Samatvam](/posts/boundaries-as-compassion/), or equanimity. Instead of asking, "Please make this happen," the prayer becomes, "Help me be the person who can handle whatever happens with grace."

## Food for Thought

> If you knew with certainty that the universe was already working for your highest evolution then how would your prayers change tonight?
>
> Would you still ask for a different path or would you ask for the eyes to see the beauty of the one you are currently on?
