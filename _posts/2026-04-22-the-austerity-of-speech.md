> Is “radical honesty” or being “brutally truthful” a spiritual shortcut?
{: .prompt-tip }

## Vachas Tapas

The *Bhagavad Gita* gives us the idea of **Vachas Tapas**, or the austerity of speech. For [speech](/posts/speaking-truth-without-ego/) to be considered as a disciplined, spiritual act, it must be: non-agitating, truthful, and beneficial.

## Brutal Truth

If we speak a truth that is factually accurate but deliver it in a way that causes unnecessary agitation, we may have failed the austerity of speech. Why? 

Because the purpose of Dharmic communication is not simply to dump data into another person’s lap. The purpose is to support clarity, growth, and alignment. If our bluntness causes the other person to shut down, become defensive, or collapse into despair, then we may not have delivered truth. We may have only struck them with it. The truth may have been lost in the violence of the delivery.

## The Ego Behind Bluntness

We should pause and examine the Gunas (qualities of nature) behind the desire to be blunt. If you find yourself saying, *"I’m just a blunt person, I don't care how they feel,"* that indifference is often a sign of Rajas (passion/ego) or Tamas (inertia/insensitivity). Rajasic speech is often driven by a subtle desire to feel powerful or superior through hard truths.

## The Labor of Austerity

True speech requires effort. It is hard to be truthful and compassionate at the same time.

It is easy to say:

> “I just tell it like it is.”

It is harder to ask:

> “How can I speak this truth in a way that gives it the best chance of being received?”
{: .prompt-tip }

Austerity of speech requires restraint, intelligence, and care. It asks us to purify not only what we say, but why and how we say it.

## Learning

The *Bhagavad Gita* suggests that the manner in which we speak is a reflection of our own internal state. If you truly see the Divine Self in the other person then you cannot be indifferent to their feelings. In short, the nicer way isn't about sugar-coating rather it’s about ensuring the medicine of truth actually reaches the patient’s heart instead of being spat out because it was too bitter to swallow.

## Food for Thought

> When you claim you “do not care” if the truth is harsh, is that a sign of your commitment to truth?
>
> Or is it a convenient excuse to avoid the difficult emotional labor of empathy?
