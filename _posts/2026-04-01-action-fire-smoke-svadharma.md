> If everyone has their own “truth” or version of what is “right,” how can any action be truly righteous?
{: .prompt-tip }

## Action and Smoke

I’ve been reflecting on a powerful analogy from the *Bhagavad Gita*:

> Every action is like a fire, and every fire has smoke.

No action is completely free from imperfection. Every choice we make can be misunderstood, resisted, or seen differently by someone else. Even when the intention is sincere there may still be some “smoke.”
- Being honest might cause temporary pain.
- Setting a boundary might look like being “mean” to someone else.
- Choosing one duty may mean disappointing another expectation.
- Speaking up may create discomfort.
- Staying silent may also create consequences.

The *Bhagavad Gita* reminds us *not to be paralyzed by the smoke*. If we wait for an action that has no possible flaw, no possible consequence, and no possibility of being misunderstood, we may never act at all.

## Fire (Intention)

Instead of obsessing over whether an action will look perfect to everyone, the *Bhagavad Gita* asks us to examine the fire behind the action.

What is intention? 
- Am I acting from clarity, duty, and sincerity? OR
- Am I acting from ego, fear, comfort, or the desire to be seen as right?

This is where [Witness Consciousness](/posts/witness-consciousness-and-authentic-action/) becomes important. It allows us to step back and observe whether our ego has quietly hijacked the wheel.

## Svadharma

**Svadharma** means acting according to one’s own duty, nature, and highest understanding of truth. This does not mean doing whatever we feel like doing. It means acting according to the highest truth we are capable of seeing in that moment.

## Learning

A “perfect action” is not one that receives universal approval. A perfect action is one where we act according to our duty, give our sincere effort, and then release attachment to the outcome. This is where [Authentic Action](/posts/witness-consciousness-and-authentic-action/) connects with **Svadharma**. We act because the action is aligned with what we understand to be right.

The *Bhagavad Gita* teaches us that we are responsible for acting according to the highest truth we can see, while using **Witness Consciousness** to ensure that the action is not being driven by ego.

We cannot fully control how others interpret our actions but we can examine the intention behind them.

## Food for Thought

> If you knew that no matter what you chose, someone would misunderstand or disagree, how would that change your decision-making?
>
> Would you still do the “right” thing if the “smoke” was guaranteed?
