> Why does our pursuit of stability often leave us most exposed to the storms of life?
{: .prompt-tip }

## Impermanence

Everything we experience through the senses is subject to **change**.

> What we see changes. What we touch changes. What we hear changes. What we taste changes. What we smell changes. Relationships shift, vacations end, career highs fade, achievements become normal, and even the emotional intensity of grief, excitement, success, and disappointment changes over time.

The problem is not that life changes. The deeper problem is that we often expect changing things to provide permanent stability. Much of our emotional suffering begins when we mistake a passing experience for the final destination or when we ask something temporary to carry the weight of permanence.

## The Bhagavad Gita’s Teaching

The *Bhagavad Gita* gives a direct teaching on this point. Krishna explains that contact between the senses and their objects gives rise to experiences of pleasure and pain. However, these experiences are temporary which come and go like winter and summer. This teaching is simple but not easy to practice. Krishna does not instruct us to control the seasons of life because the seasons are not fully within our control. Instead, He teaches us to endure them with steadiness without allowing every changing condition to disturb the inner foundation of who we are.

So Krishna’s instruction is not:

> “Control the seasons.”

It is:

> “Learn to endure them without being disturbed.”
{: .prompt-tip }

## The Wrong Kind of Stability

We usually seek stability by trying to **control external conditions**.

> We want the relationship to remain exactly as it is. We want the career to keep rising without interruption. We want the body to remain youthful, the mood to remain high, and the world to stop surprising us in ways we did not choose.

But this form of stability is fragile because the material world is dynamic by nature. To demand permanence from impermanent things is to create suffering for ourselves. The more tightly we grip what is changing, the more painfully we experience change when it inevitably moves through our hands. This does not mean that relationships, careers, health, achievements, or joyful experiences are meaningless. They can be appreciated, cared for, and honored, but they cannot become the ultimate source of our stability.

## Learning

The world will not stop changing so that we can feel safe. If our peace depends entirely on external conditions staying the same then our peace will always remain vulnerable. True stability is found by anchoring in the [Witness Consciousness](/posts/witness-consciousness-and-authentic-action/) within. You adapt because the scenery changes but you remain steady because the traveler within is not the scenery. The outer scenery continues to change but the inner Witness is not identical with the scenery. We adapt because circumstances change, yet we remain steady because the deepest part of us is not reduced to those circumstances.

## Food for Thought

> If you fully accepted that everything perceived by the senses is meant to shift, what are you currently gripping so tightly that it is causing you pain?
>
> What would change if you stopped asking that temporary thing to become permanent?
